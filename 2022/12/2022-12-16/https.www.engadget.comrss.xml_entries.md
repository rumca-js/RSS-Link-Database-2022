<p>Google, Apple and Mozilla are collaborating on a better web browser benchmark. <p>The National Highway Transportation Safety Administration announced Thursday that it is opening an investigation into the self-driving technology behind General Motors' robotaxi fleet. This announcement <a href="https://www.reuters.com/technology/us-opens-safety-probe-into-autonomous-driving-system-gms-cruise-vehicles-2022-12-16">follows three reported accidents allegedly caused by Cruise vehicles</a> braking hard or otherwise becoming immobilized in traffic, creating unannounced obstacles for other vehicles and resulting in rear-end collisions with other motorists.</p><p>&quot;With respect to the incidents of hard braking, NHTSA has received three reports of the ADS initiating a hard braking maneuver in response to another road user that was quickly approaching from the rear,&quot; <a href="https://static.nhtsa.gov/odi/inv/2022/INOA-PE22014-4871.PDF">the agency reports</a>, noting that human supervisors were aboard for each incident. &quot;In each case, the other road user subsequently struck the rear of the ADS-equipped vehicle.&quot;</p><span id="end-legacy-contents"></span><p>&quot;With respect to the incidents of vehicle immobilization, NHTSA has been notified of multiple reports involving Cruise ADS equipped vehicles, operating without onboard human supervision, becoming immobilized,&quot; the report continues. &quot;When this occurs, the vehicle may strand vehicle passengers in unsafe locations, such as lanes of travel or intersections, and become an unexpected obstacle to other road users.&quot;</p><p>In response the company touted its technology's history of safe operations. ‚ÄúCruise‚Äôs safety record is publicly reported and includes having driven nearly 700,000 fully autonomous miles in an extremely complex urban environment with zero life-threatening injuries or fatalities,&quot; Hannah Lindow, Cruise spokesperson, told Engadget via email. &quot;This is against the backdrop of over 40,000 deaths each year on American roads. The company began <a href="https://www.engadget.com/gm-cruise-california-passengers-fully-driverless-rides-161005084.html">testing ADS rides in San Francisco</a> in June, 2021 and <a href="https://www.engadget.com/cruise-charge-fully-driverless-rides-san-francisco-092000547.html">earlier this year earned regulatory approval to charge for driverless taxi services</a> within the city.&nbsp;</p><p>The company has also suffered setbacks in its pursuit of self-driving taxis. <a href="https://www.engadget.com/cruise-ceo-to-step-down-as-gm-accelerates-self-driving-car-plans-221238170.html">Division CEO Dan Ammann stepped down from his position in June</a>, replaced for the interim by CTO and founder Kyle Vogt. ## Extreme weather leads to more negative tweets, study finds
 - [https://www.engadget.com/extreme-weather-negative-tweet-sentiment-study-175149149.html?src=rss](https://www.engadget.com/extreme-weather-negative-tweet-sentiment-study-175149149.html?src=rss)
 - RSS feed: https://www.engadget.com/rss.xml
 - date published: 2022-12-16 17:51:49+00:00

<p>If it's ever seemed like people are more crotchety on social media when there's a heatwave or heavy rain, you're probably not alone in having that perspective. Researchers analyzed more than 7.7 billion geotagged <a href="https://www.engadget.com/tag/twitter/"><ins>tweets</ins></a> from 190 countries that were posted between 2015 and 2021. They used a <a href="https://www.liwc.app/demo/"><ins>language analysis tool</ins></a> to measure the sentiment of tweets against daily weather data.</p><p>The researchers <a href="https://agu.confex.com/agu/fm22/meetingapp.cgi/Paper/1066021">found that</a>, compared with days of regular weather, &quot;both local extreme heat and extreme precipitation events worsen online emotional states globally by elevating rates of posts with negative expressions and also reducing the rate of posts with positive words.&quot; They also determined that people were more likely to tweet negatively during downpours and heatwaves than when daylight savings time kicks in and they forego an hour of sleep.</p><span id="end-legacy-contents"></span><p>These outcomes might not seem incredibly surprising. However, the researchers suggested that because the findings were so consistent across tweets from more than 43,000 counties, they indicate that we're finding it hard to adapt to climate change. They carried out the study in the first place to explore the links between climate change and mental health.</p><p>‚ÄúAs of right now, we see very little evidence of adaptation in the way that these new extreme events that are emerging globally are impacting human sentiment,‚Äù says Kelton Minor, a postdoctoral research scientist at Columbia University and co-author of the study, told <a href="https://www.theverge.com/2022/12/16/23511373/extreme-weather-social-media-twitter-sentiment"><em><ins>The Verge</ins></em></a>. &quot;Since climate change is shifting the extreme tails of most regional temperature and heavy precipitation distributions rightwards, the impact of more severe extremes on overt emotional states may far exceed those registered in the recent past, pending further adaptation,&quot; the abstract of the study reads.</p><p>Minor and co-author Nick Obradovich, chief scientist at a nonprofit called Project Regeneration, found the biggest shift in sentiment during a record-breaking heatwave in the Pacific Northwest and southwest Canada in 2021. More than a thousand deaths were linked to that heatwave, while negative sentiment in tweets increased tenfold compared with the typical heatwave in the US, the researchers found. Minor and his colleagues plan to keep monitoring social media sentiment in the face of more extreme weather events, which studies suggest are likely to <a href="https://www.engadget.com/2017-06-19-research-deadly-heatwaves-often.html/"><ins>happen more often</ins></a> amid <a href="https://www.engadget.com/un-emissions-gap-report-climate-change-warning-144323723.html/"><ins>rising global temperatures</ins></a>.</p>
